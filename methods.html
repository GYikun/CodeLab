Our methods

Three ways of reading behaviour

Each method captures a different layer of the same signal — how the brain shapes behaviour, and how behaviour reveals the brain.

01

Tappigraphy

Continuous, passive capture of the timing, rhythm and dynamics of everyday smartphone taps and swipes — a behavioural signal that never stops recording, even when the lab is closed.

Touch dynamics Passive capture Continuous data
02

Neural recording

From intracranial brain implants in clinical populations to laboratory EEG tasks — linking real brain activity to real-world touchscreen behaviour.

Brain implants EEG Clinical populations
03

Computational & complexity science

Machine learning and nonlinear dynamics applied to large-scale behavioural time series — revealing ageing, disease and rhythmic patterns invisible to the naked eye.

Machine learning Complexity science Large-scale data

Evidence

What we've found so far

Touch dynamics reveal resilience in ageing

The rhythm of everyday taps and swipes stays remarkably stable as people age, exposing a form of behavioural resilience invisible to standard cognitive tests.

Ceolini, Ridderinkhof & Ghosh — PNAS (2024)

Multi-day rhythms hide in smartphone use

Smartphone behaviour follows shared rhythms that repeat across several days, not just within a single day — a pattern common across many users.

Ceolini & Ghosh — npj Digital Medicine (2023)

AI detects epilepsy from touch behaviour alone

A neural network trained only on smartphone touchscreen behaviour can trace epileptiform activity, without any direct brain recording.

Duckrow, Ceolini, Zaveri, Brooks & Ghosh — iScience (2021)

Smartphone data flags neurological disease

A model of healthy ageing built from everyday smartphone interactions reveals an advanced "behavioural age" in people with neurological disease.

Ceolini, Brunner, Bunschoten, Majoie, Thijs & Ghosh — iScience (2022)

Sleep behaviour has a stable, personal signature

Combining wearables, phone use and self-reports uncovers trait-like patterns in nighttime sleep that stay stable across time for each person.

Massar, Chua, Soon, Ng, Ong, Chee, Lee, Ghosh & Chee — npj Digital Medicine (2021)

Dopamine capacity mirrors social phone use

The brain's striatal dopamine synthesis capacity is reflected in how socially active a person is on their smartphone.

Westbrook, Ghosh, van den Bosch, Määttä, Hofmans & Cools — iScience (2021)
